QQQ — Secondary Market Trading & How Invesco Earns

Who trades QQQ on the exchange, how prices move, and the key question: does Invesco make money from secondary market trading — or only from the annual fee?

How QQQ trades in the secondary market

Once QQQ shares exist — created via the AP process — they trade freely on Nasdaq between investors. Invesco plays no role in this trading. It is exactly like buying and selling any other share.

💡 The vast majority of QQQ volume — often 95%+ on a normal day — is pure secondary market trading between investors. No new shares are created. Invesco's fund assets are completely unaffected. Only the shareholder register changes.

Who trades QQQ and why

QQQ's massive daily volume (~$15–20B) comes from a diverse mix of participants — each with very different holding periods and motivations.

💡 QQQ's extreme liquidity (1-cent spread, $15–20B daily volume) makes it the instrument of choice for anyone wanting quick, cheap exposure to US tech. This liquidity benefits Invesco too — it makes QQQ more attractive and grows its AUM, which grows the fee income.

How QQQ's price moves through the trading day

QQQ's price is set continuously by supply and demand on Nasdaq — but it is tethered to the value of its underlying holdings by the AP arbitrage mechanism.

💡 QQQ's price is essentially a real-time vote on the value of 101 large tech companies. Because APs continuously arbitrage any gaps, QQQ is one of the most accurately priced instruments in global financial markets — more accurate than many individual stocks.

Where Invesco earns — and where it does not

This is the most important and often misunderstood point: Invesco earns nothing from secondary market trading. Its only revenue from QQQ is the annual management fee charged on assets.

Revenue source Invesco earns? Detail
Management fee (expense ratio) ✓ YES 0.20% per year on total AUM, charged daily as a tiny reduction in NAV. At $290B AUM ≈ $580M/year. This is Invesco's entire QQQ revenue.
Secondary market trading (bid/ask spread) ✗ NO The spread is earned by market makers (Citadel, Virtu etc.), not Invesco. When you pay $480.01 bid and sell at $480.00 ask, that $0.01 goes to the market maker.
Creation / redemption fees ✓ small fee Invesco charges APs a small transaction fee per creation/redemption (typically $500–$2,000 per creation unit). This covers admin costs — it is immaterial versus the management fee revenue.
Securities lending ✗ NO (QQQ) QQQ is structured as a Unit Investment Trust — it legally cannot lend its securities. Newer Invesco ETFs (e.g. QQQM) are open-end funds and do lend securities, passing income to investors.
Dividend income ✗ NO Dividends received from the underlying stocks pass through to QQQ shareholders quarterly. Invesco takes no cut — the management fee is already charged on top of the gross assets.
Capital gains from rising stocks ✗ NO When Apple or Nvidia rises, the fund's NAV grows — but Invesco doesn't keep that gain. It belongs to the shareholders. Invesco's fee is a percentage of NAV, so it benefits indirectly (higher AUM = higher fee in dollar terms).
⚠️ Common misconception: because QQQ trades $15–20B per day, people assume Invesco earns from that activity. It doesn't. Every cent of trading revenue goes to market makers, brokers, and exchanges — not to Invesco. Invesco only earns the 0.20% annual fee on the $290B it holds.
