Broadridge — The Hidden Infrastructure of ETF Markets

Most people have never heard of Broadridge. Yet it touches almost every ETF transaction in the US — processing trades, delivering fund documents, routing proxy votes, and tracking who owns what. It is the operating system running silently beneath the markets.

Who is Broadridge and why does it matter for ETFs?

Broadridge Financial Solutions (NYSE: BR) is a fintech company that provides the back-office infrastructure most large financial firms outsource. It is one of the most systemically important companies in US capital markets — and one of the least visible to the public.

💡 Broadridge was spun out of ADP (the payroll company) in 2007. ADP had built brokerage back-office services as a side business in the 1960s — and by the time it spun off, that side business had become the plumbing of Wall Street. Today Broadridge serves 21 of 25 US fixed income primary dealers and 14 of 15 of the largest US wealth providers.
⚠️ Systemic importance: because so many firms outsource their back-office to Broadridge, a Broadridge outage would be felt across the entire US financial system simultaneously. The SEC and FINRA monitor Broadridge as a critical financial market utility alongside the DTCC.

Post-trade processing — what happens after your QQQ trade executes

When you buy or sell QQQ on Nasdaq, the trade is matched in microseconds — but then a complex sequence of back-office steps must happen before the shares and cash actually change hands. Broadridge runs most of this for the industry.

💡 The T+1 settlement cycle — moved from T+2 in May 2024 — compressed the window Broadridge has to match and affirm trades from the next morning to 9 PM on trade date. Broadridge built the industry infrastructure to handle this across 120 million accounts simultaneously.
📋 For ETF creation/redemption specifically: Broadridge's systems also handle the DTCC's ETF creation and redemption workflow — the back-office matching when an AP delivers a basket of stocks to Invesco in exchange for new QQQ units. The DTCC's Universal Trade Capture (UTC) system, which Broadridge interfaces with, assigns the settlement date for these transactions.

Investor communications — the legal pipeline between Invesco and you

Invesco is legally required to send regulatory documents to every QQQ shareholder. But Invesco doesn't know who you are — your broker does. Broadridge solves this problem for the entire industry.

Document type 01
Prospectus and fund updates
When Invesco changes QQQ's fees, trustees, or investment policy, every investor must be notified. Broadridge delivers this to millions of accounts simultaneously — in the correct format for each broker's platform.
Document type 02
Annual and semi-annual reports
Required by the SEC. Covers QQQ's performance, holdings, costs, and NAV history for the period. Broadridge processes 2 billion regulatory fund communications per year across all funds it serves.
Document type 03
Tax documents (1099-DIV)
Each year, investors in QQQ receive a 1099-DIV showing any dividend income passed through from the underlying stocks. Broadridge manages the production and delivery of these across millions of accounts with different tax situations.
Document type 04
Householding and preference
If two people at the same address both hold QQQ, Broadridge can consolidate the mailing into one envelope ("householding") — saving cost. Investors can set preferences for paper vs digital through Broadridge's portal.
💡 The "street name" problem: when you hold QQQ through Fidelity, the shares are technically registered to "Fidelity" at the DTC — not to you personally. You are a "beneficial owner." Broadridge's entire communications business exists to solve this problem — bridging the gap between the registered holder (the broker) and the real economic owner (you).

Proxy voting — Broadridge's near-monopoly in corporate governance

The stocks inside QQQ — Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia and 98 others — all hold annual shareholder meetings. Those votes must be cast by whoever holds the shares. QQQ holds the shares. Broadridge routes the votes.

⚠️ Near-monopoly: A 2010 US House Committee report noted Broadridge's near-monopoly position in proxy voting infrastructure. It remains the dominant provider today — connecting over 200 million investors to virtually every public company, mutual fund, and ETF. This concentration is both efficient and a systemic risk regulators monitor closely.
💡 Because QQQ is a Unit Investment Trust, Invesco must vote the underlying stocks — it cannot abstain. Broadridge's ProxyEdge platform is what Invesco's stewardship team uses to manage hundreds of proxy votes per year across every stock in QQQ's portfolio.

Distribution analytics — Broadridge's intelligence layer for asset managers

Beyond operations, Broadridge sits on a uniquely valuable dataset: because it processes the back-office for most brokers, it can see — in aggregate — exactly where money is flowing into and out of every fund. Asset managers like Invesco pay for this intelligence.

Analytics product 01
Fund flow intelligence
Broadridge tracks net inflows and outflows across every fund and every distribution channel — by broker, by advisor, by region. Invesco uses this to see which Fidelity advisors are growing QQQ positions and which are switching to a rival ETF.
Analytics product 02
Advisor-level data
Broadridge's data shows which individual financial advisors hold which ETFs across their books. This is commercially sensitive intelligence that Invesco's wholesalers use to prioritise sales calls and measure distribution effectiveness.
Analytics product 03
Competitive positioning
Invesco can see — via Broadridge — how QQQ's flows compare to BlackRock's QQQ-equivalent, Fidelity's ONEQ, and every other Nasdaq-100 tracker. This market intelligence doesn't exist in any public source — it comes from Broadridge's unique position in the plumbing.
Analytics product 04
Active ETF launch support
For new ETF launches, Broadridge provides market sizing, target advisor profiling, and early flow monitoring. Its 2025 Active ETF report — drawing on actual distribution data — showed only 11% of active ETFs launched in the past three years raised over $100M in their first year.
💡 This is Broadridge's most strategically valuable — and least discussed — asset. Because it processes the back-office for most of the US broker industry, it has a unique cross-market view of where every dollar is flowing. No other data vendor can match it, because no other vendor is actually inside the plumbing at the same depth.

Where Broadridge fits across the full ETF lifecycle

Pulling it all together — where Broadridge appears in each stage of the ETF journey covered in this series, and what it does not touch.

Stage in ETF lifecycle Broadridge involved? What Broadridge does
Fund creation (Part 1) Peripheral Invesco uses Broadridge for fund accounting and regulatory filing support. The creation unit mechanism itself runs through DTCC, not Broadridge directly.
AP creation/redemption (Part 3) Yes — settlement Broadridge's systems interface with DTCC's ETF creation/redemption workflow, handling the back-office settlement of the in-kind basket exchange between the AP and Invesco's custodian.
Secondary market trading (Parts 2 & 4) Yes — post-trade Broadridge processes the post-trade workflow for most broker-dealers — trade capture, matching, affirmation, and submission to DTCC for T+1 settlement. It does not touch the trade execution itself.
Exchange order matching (Part 2) Not involved Nasdaq, NYSE Arca, and CBOE run their own matching engines. Broadridge has no role in order routing, price discovery, or trade execution. That is the exchange's domain.
Investor communications (Part 5) Yes — dominant Broadridge is the primary infrastructure for delivering prospectuses, annual reports, tax documents, and proxy materials from Invesco to QQQ's millions of beneficial owners through the broker network.
Proxy voting — underlying stocks Yes — near-monopoly Every AGM vote that Invesco casts on QQQ's Apple, Microsoft, and Nvidia shares routes through Broadridge's ProxyEdge platform. Broadridge tallies the votes and submits them back to the issuing company.
Market data and monitoring (Part 5) Yes — analytics Broadridge's distribution data and analytics platform gives Invesco unique insight into where QQQ ownership is concentrated, how flows compare to rivals, and which distribution channels are growing or shrinking.
Invesco's fee collection Not involved The 0.20% expense ratio is collected by State Street (the custodian) as a daily NAV reduction and remitted to Invesco. Broadridge is not part of this flow.
🔭 Looking ahead: Broadridge is actively building blockchain infrastructure for tokenised securities. It is a participant in the DTCC's tokenisation working group alongside Invesco, BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, and Citadel. If ETF shares are eventually tokenised on-chain, Broadridge intends to provide the same post-trade, communications, and governance infrastructure it provides today — but on distributed ledger rails.
💡 The simplest way to think about Broadridge: it is the shared back-office that the financial industry outsources to instead of each firm building their own. Every time a QQQ trade settles, a fund document gets delivered, a proxy vote gets cast, or a distribution team checks their flow data — Broadridge is very likely the infrastructure making it happen.
