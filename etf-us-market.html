US ETF Secondary Market Infrastructure

Who runs the exchanges, how your order routes, what dark pools actually are, and the regulation that ties it all together.

The US exchange landscape for ETF trading

There are 16 registered national securities exchanges in the US. Three dominate ETF listings — NYSE Arca, Nasdaq, and CBOE BZX. An ETF listed on any one of them can trade on all 16 simultaneously.

How a retail ETF order routes through the US market

From your brokerage app to execution — every stop your order makes in under a millisecond.

💡 The Smart Order Router checks prices across all 16 exchanges plus off-exchange venues in microseconds. By law (Reg NMS Order Protection Rule), your broker must get you the best available price — the National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO).

Dark pools and OTC — the off-exchange world

About 40% of US ETF volume trades away from public exchanges. This isn't sinister — it solves a real problem for large institutional orders that would otherwise move the market against themselves.

💡 "Dark" refers to pre-trade transparency, not regulation. All dark pools are registered with the SEC as Alternative Trading Systems (ATS) and must report every trade to FINRA within 10 seconds of execution.

Reg NMS — the rule that unifies the US market

Regulation National Market System (SEC, 2005) created a single unified price across all 16 competing exchanges. Its core requirement: every order must be filled at the National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO) or better — no exceptions.

💡 High-frequency traders pay for faster "proprietary feeds" directly from each exchange (bypassing the SIP), giving them a microsecond edge. This is controversial but legal — and is one reason IEX introduced its 350µs "speed bump" to level the playing field.
