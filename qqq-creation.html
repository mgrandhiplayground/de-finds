Invesco QQQ — How New Units Are Created

How Invesco, as fund manager, issues new QQQ shares into the market — from the Nasdaq-100 index basket to the creation unit process and the role of authorised participants.

What is Invesco QQQ?

QQQ is one of the most traded securities on the planet. It tracks the Nasdaq-100 — the 100 largest non-financial companies listed on the Nasdaq exchange — and is managed by Invesco Ltd.

💡 QQQ was originally created by Nasdaq itself in 1999 ("Cubes" — from its QQQ ticker). Invesco acquired the management rights in 2007. It is structured as a Unit Investment Trust (UIT), not a typical open-end fund — meaning it must fully replicate the index with no discretion.

The Nasdaq-100 basket — what QQQ actually holds

QQQ must hold exactly the same stocks, in exactly the same proportions, as the Nasdaq-100 index. Nasdaq Inc. calculates and publishes the index. Invesco must follow it — with no deviation.

💡 QQQ is structured as a Unit Investment Trust — the oldest ETF legal wrapper. Unlike newer ETFs, it cannot lend securities or hold derivatives. It must hold every stock in the index at all times, making it a pure, passive tracker.

The QQQ creation unit process — step by step

New QQQ shares only come into existence through a formal creation unit process. Invesco publishes the exact basket each morning; authorised participants assemble it and deliver it in exchange for new units.

  • 1
    Invesco publishes the creation basket ~9:00 AM ET
    Each morning before market open, Invesco publishes the exact list of stocks and quantities required to create one QQQ creation unit (50,000 shares). This "portfolio composition file" (PCF) is public — any AP can see it. It reflects the current Nasdaq-100 weights adjusted for corporate actions overnight.
  • 2
    AP assembles the basket during trading day
    An authorised participant (e.g. Goldman Sachs, Citadel, Jane Street) purchases every stock in the basket in the exact required proportions on the open market. For one QQQ creation unit of 50,000 shares, the basket value is approximately $24 million at current prices.
  • 3
    AP delivers basket to custodian by 3:00 PM ET
    The AP delivers the assembled basket of stocks to State Street Bank (QQQ's custodian) via the DTCC (Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation). The DTCC settles the in-kind transfer — no cash changes hands for the securities leg. A small cash component covers dividends accrued and any fractional share adjustments.
  • 4
    Invesco issues 50,000 new QQQ shares T+1 settlement
    Once custodian confirms receipt of the basket, Invesco instructs the transfer agent to create 50,000 new QQQ shares and credit them to the AP's account. These shares are new — they did not exist before. Total QQQ shares outstanding increases by 50,000.
  • 5
    AP sells units on exchange profit opportunity
    The AP now owns 50,000 QQQ shares and can sell them on Nasdaq to retail and institutional investors. The AP profits from the difference between the cost of assembling the basket and the price at which QQQ shares trade — typically a few cents per share on the spread.
💡 Multiple creation units can be created on the same day — if demand is high and QQQ's price rises above its NAV, APs will keep creating units until the gap is closed. On very busy days, hundreds of millions of dollars of new units can be created.

Invesco's role — what the fund manager actually does

Invesco is responsible for the fund's legal structure, compliance, and ongoing operations — but it does not actively pick stocks. Its job is accurate replication and fund governance.

💡 Invesco earns its 0.20% management fee regardless of whether QQQ goes up or down — it is charged on the total assets held in the fund. At ~$290B AUM this generates approximately $580M per year in fee revenue for Invesco. This is their only income from QQQ — they do not profit from secondary market trading.

The redemption process — how units leave circulation

Redemption is the mirror image of creation. An AP returns 50,000 QQQ shares to Invesco and receives the underlying basket of stocks back. This keeps supply and demand in balance.

  • 1
    QQQ trades at a discount to NAV trigger
    If QQQ's market price falls below its net asset value (the sum of its holdings), an AP can profit by buying QQQ shares cheaply on the exchange and redeeming them for the more valuable basket of stocks.
  • 2
    AP buys 50,000 QQQ shares on exchange market purchase
    The AP purchases 50,000 QQQ shares in the open market at the discounted price. This buying pressure itself starts pushing QQQ's price back toward NAV.
  • 3
    AP submits redemption order to Invesco by 3:00 PM ET
    The AP delivers 50,000 QQQ shares to State Street (the custodian). Invesco cancels these shares — total QQQ shares outstanding decreases by 50,000. These shares are destroyed, not resold.
  • 4
    AP receives the basket of stocks T+1 settlement
    Invesco instructs the custodian to transfer the underlying stocks (Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia etc.) to the AP's account. The AP now holds the individual stocks, which are worth more than what it paid for QQQ — that gap is the profit.
  • 5
    AP sells the individual stocks realising profit
    The AP sells the individual stocks on the open market. This selling pressure on the underlying stocks helps bring them slightly down, while the earlier QQQ buying pushed it slightly up — together, this closes the gap between QQQ price and NAV.
⚖️ Creation and redemption together act as an automatic stabiliser — they are the reason QQQ's price almost never diverges from its NAV by more than a fraction of a cent. The AP's profit motive does the stabilisation for free.
💡 The in-kind exchange (stocks for units, not cash) is the key tax advantage of ETFs. When stocks are redeemed out of the fund, Invesco doesn't need to sell anything — so no capital gains are realised inside the fund. QQQ has distributed almost no capital gains to investors in its 25-year history.
