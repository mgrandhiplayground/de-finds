How ETF Markets Work

From fund creation to secondary market trading — a visual walkthrough of the full ETF lifecycle.

What is an ETF?

A fund that tracks an index and trades like a share on a stock exchange — combining diversification with intraday liquidity.

💡 Unlike a mutual fund (priced once daily at NAV), an ETF trades throughout the day at market prices — giving investors flexibility and real-time pricing.

Creation units — how the fund manager issues ETF shares

ETFs are created in large blocks called "creation units" — typically 25,000 to 200,000 shares at a time. New ETF shares only enter circulation this way.

💡 The creation/redemption process is "in-kind" — securities are exchanged for ETF shares, not cash. This keeps prices aligned to NAV and makes ETFs highly tax-efficient.

Authorised participants — the key intermediaries

Only ~30–40 large financial firms globally are registered APs, allowed to create and redeem units directly with the fund manager.

💡 APs are the "plumbing" of the ETF market. Without them, ETF prices could drift far from underlying asset values. They profit from small price differences — and in doing so, keep the ETF price honest.

Secondary market — how ETFs trade between investors

The vast majority of ETF trading never involves the fund manager — it's simply existing units changing hands on the exchange, like any other share.

💡 Because ETFs trade like shares, you can buy or sell at any time the exchange is open — including limit orders, stop-losses, and short selling. Mutual funds only let you transact at the end-of-day NAV.

Arbitrage — what keeps the ETF price honest

If the ETF's market price drifts from its underlying NAV, APs step in to profit — and in doing so, instantly correct the gap.

💡 This self-correcting mechanism is elegant — it's why ETFs have displaced many actively managed mutual funds over the past two decades. Efficiency is built into the structure.
